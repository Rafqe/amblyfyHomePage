Traditional Treatments

1. Corrective Eyewear

The first step in treating amblyopia often involves correcting any refractive errors such as nearsightedness, farsightedness, or astigmatism with prescription glasses or contact lenses. In some cases, clear vision can be restored solely through consistent use of proper eyewear.

2. Patching Therapy (Occlusion Therapy)

This is the most widely used traditional method. A patch is placed over the stronger eye, forcing the brain to rely on the weaker eye. Over time, this encourages the development of visual pathways in the brain corresponding to the amblyopic eye. Patching schedules can vary, often ranging from a few hours a day to full-time wear, depending on the severity and age of the patient.

3. Atropine Eye Drops

An alternative to patching, atropine drops are placed in the stronger eye to temporarily blur vision, again forcing the brain to use the weaker eye. This method is particularly useful for children who may resist wearing an eye patch.

4. Vision Therapy (Orthoptics)

In some cases, structured eye exercises or vision therapy may be prescribed by an eye care professional. These exercises help improve coordination, focusing, and binocular vision. Traditional vision therapy may be done at home or in a clinical setting.

5. Surgical Options (if necessary)

If amblyopia is caused by conditions such as strabismus (eye misalignment) or ptosis (drooping eyelid), surgery may be required to correct the anatomical issue before or during amblyopia treatment.

Note: Traditional amblyopia treatment is most effective when started early in life. While treatment in older children and adults may still offer improvements, the degree of visual recovery tends to be lower. Regular follow-ups with an eye care professional are essential to monitor progress and adjust treatment as needed.
